Overview

Battleship is a two-player guessing and deduction game.
Each player secretly places a fleet of ships on their own grid, then takes turns calling out coordinates to try to hit and sink the opponent’s ships.

The winner is the first player to sink all of the opponent’s ships.

What comes in the game

A standard Battleship setup includes for each player:

1 ocean grid where you place your own ships

1 target grid where you record your shots against the opponent

5 ships

The traditional fleet is:

Carrier – length 5

Battleship – length 4

Cruiser – length 3

Submarine – length 3

Destroyer – length 2

“Length” means how many grid spaces the ship occupies.

The board

Each player has a 10 × 10 grid.

The rows are usually labeled with letters:

A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H, I, J

The columns are labeled with numbers:

1 through 10

So each square has a coordinate, such as:

A1

B7

J10

Objective

Your goal is to:

Find where your opponent placed their ships

Hit every space occupied by those ships

Sink all 5 of their ships before they sink yours

Setup
1. Sit facing each other

Each player should be able to see only their own boards.
Your ship positions must remain hidden from your opponent.

2. Place your ships on your ocean grid

Each ship must be placed on the grid before the game starts.

Placement rules

Each ship:

Must be placed entirely within the grid

Must be placed in a straight line

Can be placed either:

Horizontally

Vertically

Cannot be placed diagonally

Cannot bend or turn corners

Cannot overlap another ship

Examples

A Destroyer of length 2 could be placed:

Horizontally on C4 and C5

Vertically on C4 and D4

A Carrier of length 5 could be placed:

Horizontally from A1 to A5

Vertically from F7 to J7

Invalid placements

These are not allowed:

Part of a ship hanging off the board

A ship shaped like an L

A diagonal ship

Two ships sharing one square

3. Confirm both players are ready

Once both players finish placing ships, the game begins.

Turn order

Players decide who goes first.
This can be done however you want unless a house rule says otherwise.

Then players alternate turns, one shot per turn.

How a turn works

On your turn:

Choose one coordinate on your opponent’s board

Announce it clearly, such as:

“B6”

“J10”

Your opponent checks whether that coordinate contains one of their ships

They must answer truthfully

Possible shot results
Miss

If the called coordinate does not contain any part of a ship, the opponent says:

“Miss”

You then mark that square on your target grid as a miss.

Hit

If the called coordinate does contain part of a ship, the opponent says:

“Hit”

You mark that square on your target grid as a hit.

Sunk

If your shot hits the last remaining unhit space of one specific ship, the opponent says:

“Hit and sunk”
or

“You sunk my [ship name]”

For example:

“You sunk my Destroyer”

“You sunk my Battleship”

This matters because players are traditionally told which ship was sunk.

Important honesty rule

Battleship only works if both players respond honestly.

That means:

You must say “hit” whenever a called coordinate hits one of your ships

You must say “miss” whenever it does not

You must say when a ship has been sunk

You may not move ships once the game starts

You may not change past results

Marking the boards
Your ocean grid

This is where your own ships are placed.

You should mark:

Which parts of your ships have been hit

Which enemy shots missed

Your target grid

This is where you track your guesses against your opponent.

You should mark:

Hits

Misses

Sunk ships if you know them

The exact markers depend on the set, but commonly:

Red peg = hit

White peg = miss

What counts as sinking a ship

A ship is sunk when every square that ship occupies has been hit.

Examples:

A Destroyer occupies 2 spaces. It sinks after both spaces are hit.

A Carrier occupies 5 spaces. It sinks after all 5 spaces are hit.

A ship is not sunk just because it has been hit once or multiple times.
It sinks only when all of its spaces are hit.

End of the game

The game ends immediately when one player sinks the opponent’s final remaining ship.

That player wins.

There is no points system in the traditional version.
It is simply win or lose.

Traditional fleet details

Here is the standard fleet again with exact sizes:

Ship	Length
Carrier	5
Battleship	4
Cruiser	3
Submarine	3
Destroyer	2

A total of 17 ship squares are on each player’s board.

That means to win, you must score 17 hits, correctly distributed across all ships.

Example of play
Ship placement example

A player might place:

Carrier: A1–A5

Battleship: C7–F7

Cruiser: H2–H4

Submarine: D10–F10

Destroyer: J3–J4

These positions are secret.

Gameplay example

Player 1 says:

“E5”

Player 2 checks and says:

“Miss”

Player 1 marks E5 as a miss on their target grid.

Next turn, Player 2 says:

“A3”

Player 1 checks and says:

“Hit”

Player 2 marks A3 as a hit.

Later, if Player 2 hits A1, A2, A4, and A5 after already hitting A3, Player 1 says:

“You sunk my Carrier”

Traditional restrictions and conventions

These are the standard expectations in the classic board game.

1. One shot per turn

Normally, each player gets exactly one shot per turn.

2. No repeated shots

You generally should not call a coordinate you already called before.

In most games, if you do, one of two things happens:

It is treated as a wasted turn, or

The opponent tells you that coordinate was already called and you choose again

The cleaner traditional approach is:

Do not repeat coordinates

3. No moving ships after setup

Once the game starts, ships stay where they were placed.

4. Ships remain hidden

You do not reveal ship locations unless they are inferred through hits, or a ship is sunk.

5. Ships are not adjacent by rule unless house rules say so

In the classic game, ships may be placed directly next to each other, including side-by-side or end-to-end, as long as they do not overlap.

Some people use a house rule saying ships cannot touch, but that is not the standard traditional rule.

Common house rules that are not official standard rules

Many people grow up playing with variations. These are common, but not part of the strict traditional rules unless agreed upon beforehand.

Salvo rule

Instead of firing one shot per turn, a player fires multiple shots based on how many ships they still have afloat.

Example:

5 ships left = 5 shots

4 ships left = 4 shots

This is a variation, not the basic standard rule.

Ships cannot touch

Some people require at least one square of space between ships.
Traditional Battleship does not require this.

Announcing only “hit” or “miss,” not ship name

Some groups do not require naming the ship when sunk.
In classic Battleship, you usually do identify the sunk ship.

Continue firing after a hit

Some people let a player keep shooting until they miss.
This is also a house rule, not the normal rule.

Strategy implications of the rules

Because ships are straight and occupy multiple consecutive squares:

A single hit suggests neighboring squares may contain more of the same ship

Diagonal follow-ups are usually less useful because ships are not diagonal

Longer ships are easier to hit but harder to hide

Shorter ships are harder to find late in the game

Also, because ships can legally touch in the standard rules, a “sunk” ship does not guarantee nearby squares are empty.

Clean official-style summary

If you wanted the rules in the simplest formal version, it would be this:

Each player places 5 ships secretly on a 10×10 grid.

Ships must be straight, horizontal or vertical, entirely on the board, and non-overlapping.

Players alternate turns calling one coordinate at a time.

The opponent announces whether the shot is a miss, hit, or sinks a ship.

Each player records shots on their tracking grid.

The first player to sink all 5 enemy ships wins.

Clarification on “Battleship” the ship vs. the game

A lot of people mix these up:

Battleship = the name of the game

Battleship = also one specific ship in the fleet, length 4

So if someone says “You sunk my battleship,” they mean the 4-space ship, not that the game is over unless it was their last remaining ship.
